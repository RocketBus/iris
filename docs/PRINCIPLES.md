# PRINCIPLES.md — Iris Product Principles

## Purpose

This document defines the core principles that guide Iris.

Principles are not implementation details.
They are long-term truths that shape product and technical decisions.

If a future decision conflicts with a principle, the decision should be reconsidered.

Principles change rarely.

---

## 1. Measure Outcomes, Not Activity

Engineering activity is not equivalent to engineering impact.

More commits, pull requests, or lines of code do not necessarily produce better software outcomes.

Iris prioritizes signals that reflect durable delivery results over workflow volume.

We measure what survives, not what happens.

---

## 2. Systems Over Individuals

Software delivery is a system property.

Iris never **ranks or scores** individual developers, and never produces individual productivity metrics. Surfacing *who* contributed to a system (e.g. listing active contributors on a repo) is fine; grading, ranking, or comparing people is not.

Insights must emerge from team, repository, or organizational dynamics.

The goal is understanding systems, not judging people.

---

## 3. Observable Reality Over Opinion

Iris relies on observable signals derived from real engineering workflows.

We avoid subjective inputs such as surveys, self-reported productivity, or manual scoring whenever possible.

Reality leaves traces in systems.
We analyze those traces.

---

## 4. Insight Before Interface

Understanding precedes visualization.

A powerful insight delivered as a simple report is more valuable than a complex dashboard without meaning.

User interfaces should emerge only after analytical clarity exists.

The product’s value comes from interpretation, not presentation.

---

## 5. Simplicity Enables Discovery

Complex architectures slow learning.

During early evolution, Iris favors simple implementations that allow rapid experimentation.

Clarity of reasoning is more important than scalability.

We optimize for learning speed first.

---

## 6. Metrics Are Living Models

Metrics are models of understanding, not permanent truths.

As software development evolves, metrics must evolve as well.

All measurements in Iris are expected to be refined, challenged, and improved over time.

Rigidity is a failure mode.

---

## 7. Vendor-Agnostic Intelligence

Iris measures engineering outcomes independently of specific tools or AI vendors.

We do not depend on proprietary IDE signals or vendor telemetry.

The system analyzes behaviors and results, ensuring long-term relevance despite tooling changes.

---

## 8. Trust Is a Product Feature

Engineering analytics can easily become surveillance.

Iris must always be safe for engineering teams to adopt.

Insights must empower teams, not threaten them.

If a feature reduces trust, it should not exist.

---

## 9. Explainability Over Sophistication

Every metric must be explainable in plain language.

If an engineering leader cannot understand why a score exists, the score should not exist.

Transparent reasoning builds adoption.

Opaque intelligence creates skepticism.

---

## 10. Discover the Invisible

The purpose of Iris is to reveal dynamics that organizations cannot currently see.

The product succeeds when it makes hidden engineering patterns understandable.

We are not building better dashboards.

We are making invisible system behavior measurable.

---

## Guiding Question

When uncertain, ask:

> Does this help reveal how software delivery actually works in the AI era?

If yes, proceed.
If not, reconsider.
